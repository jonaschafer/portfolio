# Teaching a CEO to speak in brand architecture

A Series D EdTech platform serving 50M+ users had five products sharing a name, illustration style, and color system. Yet the CEO kept pushing for something the existing structure couldn't deliver—and no one could figure out why.

---

## The problem

Two persistent challenges defined my early work at this company.

The first: the CEO repeatedly left inactionable feedback. "Make it iconic." "See Stripe." "Make it trustworthy." Vague references to companies with no connective tissue to our context.

The second, and more alarming: the phrase that appeared most often in Figma comments, Slack threads, and weekly reviews was **"we need to be a house of brands."**

The underlying issue wasn't bad communication—it was a missing framework. Without understanding what brand architecture models actually mean, or how to give feedback that leads somewhere, the team was designing in the dark.

Before I arrived, the team had either ignored the feedback or attempted literal interpretations—creating divergent directions, then reverting. The cycle repeated. No one named the real problem.

---

## The approach

I tackled both issues simultaneously.

For the feedback problem, I created a framework distinguishing **critique** (problem-based, goal-aligned, user-grounded) from **reaction** (vague comparisons, subjective, unanchored). I shared it with the CEO, referenced it in project threads, and eventually rolled it out company-wide. The shift was noticeable over time—"see WhatsApp" became "this works for our brand today, but we want to go after higher education in the next few years. How do we build the brand to flex to that audience?"

For the architecture problem, I treated "house of brands" not as aesthetic feedback but as a strategic gap.

I searched Slack, Google Docs, Asana, and Figma for every instance the phrase appeared. I interviewed the team, the CMO, and marketing stakeholders. I discovered the CEO had raised the same concern during a previous agency rebrand—and it was never addressed.

What I found: **the CEO wasn't describing a brand architecture problem. He was describing a visual design problem.**

He wanted each product to feel distinct and memorable while maintaining connection. The brands all blended together. One product had no character system, no color differentiation, nothing to make it memorable on its own. When he said "house of brands," he meant: *give each product a real identity.*

---

## What I built

Rather than having another inconclusive conversation, I waited for the right moment—a project where the feedback had appeared again—and created a strategic deck explaining brand architecture models and their implications.

**The education layer**

I walked through three models:
- **House of Brands** (Procter & Gamble): Charmin and Duracell never share a world. Brands are intentionally independent.
- **Branded House** (FedEx): Everything shares the parent name and system.
- **Endorsed Model**: Products develop distinct identities while maintaining a subtle parent brand presence—think "Sparks by Dojo."

I used an analogy that landed: *"We're not Procter & Gamble where brands are intentionally siloed. We're building toward something more like how Pixar films each have distinct identities but share a recognizable universe."*

I explained why a House of Brands would weaken the trust we'd built—and why our mission required a connected system.

**The strategy**

I argued the company currently sat between Branded House and Endorsed Model, and proposed a multi-year plan:
- Remove the parent brand name from prominent positions within products
- Rename products that weren't ownable
- Engage a world-class naming agency
- Rebrand with a proper agency partner when the time was right

The Endorsed Model became the framing for "iconic"—distinct products that ladder up to a recognizable universe.

**The decision framework**

I introduced a "volume knob" concept: every brand decision considers three variables—**audience** (students vs. district admins), **surface** (Times Square billboard vs. Zoom banner), and **context** (webinar vs. product). This gave the team a systematic way to answer "how distinct should this be?" without subjective arguments.

---

## The impact

The immediate reaction was clarity and audible relief.

The long-term impact took months to surface—and that's when it mattered most.

The CEO started using "endorsed model" in Figma comments. Then Slack. Then company all-hands. He initiated conversations with a world-renowned naming agency. He began sharing articles about corporate renamings unprompted. When the cost of doing this right came up—approximately 1–2% of operating budget—he heard it, understood it, and made an informed decision to defer the full rebrand by a year while approving the internal work already underway.

**Organizational shift:** The team stopped defending aesthetic choices and started presenting decisions in architectural terms. "We made this change so each brand stands out while laddering up to the parent universe." The CEO's feedback evolved from "look at Stripe" to specific, strategic questions.

**Strategic foundation:** By naming the architecture problem, I gave the organization shared language and a multi-year roadmap. The team gained explicit permission to differentiate products while maintaining connection—and stopped trying to achieve "iconic" through design alone.

---

## What this demonstrates

**Strategic diagnosis over design solutions.** Rather than iterating on visuals, I identified that "house of brands" was a request for distinct product identities within a connected system. The real problem wasn't the architecture model—it was the absence of robust identity systems for each product.

**Education as leadership.** I built the CEO's fluency in brand architecture by connecting abstract models to familiar examples and company-specific context, while simultaneously teaching the organization how to give feedback that leads to great work. The goal wasn't winning an argument—it was creating shared understanding so leadership could make informed strategic decisions.

**Long-term thinking over quick fixes.** I proposed a multi-year roadmap with clear milestones rather than a single design sprint. The endorsed model gave the organization a north star even if full execution was years away.

**Measurable outcomes.** Feedback quality improved dramatically and sustainably. A new architectural vocabulary became embedded in how leadership talked about brand. A world-class naming agency engagement moved from abstract desire to budgeted initiative. The creative team gained strategic authority to explain decisions rather than defend them.
